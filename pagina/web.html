Introduction to Biophysics

Biophysics is an interdisciplinary science that applies approaches and methods traditionally used in physics to study biological phenomena. Biophysics covers all scales of biological organization, from molecular to organismic and populations. Biophysical research shares significant overlap with biochemistry, molecular biology, physical chemistry, physiology, nanotechnology, bioengineering, computational biology, biomechanics, developmental biology and systems biology.

The term biophysics was originally introduced by Karl Pearson in 1892.

Molecular biophysics typically addresses biological questions similar to those in biochemistry and molecular biology, seeking to find the physical underpinnings of biomolecular phenomena. Scientists in this field conduct research concerned with understanding the interactions between the various systems of a cell, including the interactions between DNA, RNA and protein biosynthesis, as well as how these interactions are regulated. A great variety of techniques are used to answer these questions.

In addition to traditional (i.e. molecular and cellular) biophysical topics like structural biology or enzyme kinetics, modern biophysics encompasses an extraordinarily broad range of research, from bioelectronics to quantum biology involving both experimental and theoretical tools. It is becoming increasingly common for biophysicists to apply the models and experimental techniques derived from physics, as well as mathematics and statistics, to larger systems such as tissues, organs, populations and ecosystems. Biophysical models are used extensively in the study of electrical conduction in single neurons, as well as neural circuit analysis in both tissue and whole brain.

Knot Theory

Knot theory is a branch of topology that studies the mathematical properties of knots and links, focusing on how they can be classified, manipulated, and transformed without cutting or breaking.

Stochastic Analysis

Stochastic analysis is the study of systems influenced by random processes, often using tools from probability theory to analyze and model phenomena in fields like finance, physics, and biology where uncertainty and randomness play a crucial role.

Gauge Theory

Gauge theory is a framework in physics and mathematics that describes the behavior of fields and forces through symmetries and transformations, particularly in the context of fundamental interactions like electromagnetism and the strong and weak nuclear forces.

Fractal Geometry

Fractal geometry studies complex structures that exhibit self-similarity at different scales, providing a way to understand irregular and fragmented shapes found in nature, such as coastlines or clouds.

Elliptic functions

Elliptic functions are complex functions that are periodic in two directions, extending the concept of trigonometric functions and playing a key role in number theory and the study of integrable systems.

Chaos Theory

Chaos theory explores the behavior of dynamic systems that are highly sensitive to initial conditions, often leading to unpredictable and complex outcomes despite being governed by deterministic rules.

Graph Theory

Graph theory is a branch of mathematics that studies the relationships between objects represented as nodes connected by edges, and is used in various fields such as computer science, biology, and social network analysis.

Differential Topology

Differential topology focuses on the properties of smooth manifolds, studying how they can be transformed and classified, particularly with respect to their differentiable structures.

Algebraic Topology

Algebraic topology uses algebraic methods to study topological spaces, focusing on concepts such as homology and fundamental groups to understand the shapes and connectivity of spaces.

Statistical mechanics

Statistical mechanics bridges the microscopic behavior of individual particles with the macroscopic properties of materials, providing insights into thermodynamics, phase transitions, and the behavior of gases and liquids.

Fluid dynamics

Fluid mechanics is the study of how fluids (liquids and gases) behave when at rest and in motion, involving principles such as viscosity, pressure, and flow dynamics.

Quantum mechanics

Quantum mechanics is the branch of physics that deals with phenomena at the atomic and subatomic levels, where particles exhibit wave-particle duality, and uncertainty plays a fundamental role in the system's behavior.

Algebraic Structures

A mathematical frameworks consisting of sets equipped with operations that satisfy specific axioms, such as groups, rings, and fields, which provide foundational tools for understanding symmetry, computation, and abstract relationships across diverse areas of mathematics and science.

Category Theory

Category theory is a branch of mathematics that studies abstract structures and relationships between them through objects and morphisms, offering a unifying language to connect and generalize concepts across algebra, topology, and computer science.

String Theory

String theory is a theoretical framework in physics that posits that the fundamental constituents of the universe are one-dimensional "strings" whose vibrations determine particle properties, aiming to unify quantum mechanics and general relativity into a single coherent model.

Omics

Omics refers to a collective field of study focused on the comprehensive analysis of biological molecules, such as genomics, proteomics, and metabolomics, aiming to understand the structure, function, and dynamics of biological systems at a holistic level.

Dynamical Systems

Dynamical systems are mathematical models that describe how points in a given space evolve over time according to deterministic or stochastic rules, providing insights into phenomena ranging from planetary motion to population dynamics.

Measure Theory

Measure theory is a branch of mathematics that investigates ways to assign consistent sizes or measures to sets, including those with infinite or irregular properties, forming the foundation for integration and probability theory.

Eukaryote

The biophysics of eukaryotes explores the physical principles governing cellular processes and structures, such as membrane dynamics, cytoskeletal mechanics, and intracellular transport, in organisms with complex, membrane-bound organelles.

Prokaryote

The biophysics of prokaryotes examines the molecular and physical mechanisms underlying cellular functions, including energy generation, motility, and genetic regulation, in unicellular organisms without membrane-bound organelles.

Viruses

The biophysics of viruses investigates the structural, mechanical, and functional properties of viral particles, focusing on processes like host-cell entry, genome packaging, and replication, as well as their interactions with the immune system.

Carcinogenesis

Carcinogenesis is the complex biological process by which normal cells transform into cancerous cells, involving genetic mutations, epigenetic alterations, and dysregulation of cellular growth and survival pathways.

General Biology

General biology is the broad scientific study of living organisms, encompassing their structure, function, evolution, behavior, and interactions with their environments across all levels of organization.

Biochemistry

Biochemistry is the study of the chemical processes and substances that occur within living organisms, focusing on the molecular basis of life, including enzymatic reactions, metabolism, and biomolecular structures.

Functional Analysis

Functional analysis is a branch of mathematics that studies vector spaces and operators acting upon them, particularly in infinite-dimensional contexts, providing foundational tools for understanding quantum mechanics, partial differential equations, and more.

Spectral Theory

Spectral theory is a mathematical field that examines the spectrum of linear operators, including eigenvalues and eigenvectors, offering deep insights into problems in quantum mechanics, signal processing, and stability analysis.

Differential Geometry

Differential geometry explores the properties of curves, surfaces, and higher-dimensional manifolds using calculus and linear algebra, serving as a cornerstone for modern physics, particularly general relativity and gauge theories.

Spinor Theory

Spinor theory investigates mathematical entities that extend the concept of vectors, offering a framework for understanding fundamental symmetries and behaviors in quantum mechanics, relativity, and particle physics.

Twistor Theory

Twistor theory is a geometric framework introduced by Roger Penrose, aiming to unify spacetime and quantum theory by representing physical fields and spacetime structures as entities in a complex projective space.

Representation Theory

Representation theory studies abstract algebraic structures, such as groups and algebras, by expressing them as linear transformations of vector spaces, bridging gaps between algebra, geometry, and physics.

Differential Equations

Differential equations describe relationships involving rates of change, capturing dynamic systems in mathematics, physics, and engineering, from fluid dynamics to population modeling.

Biophotonics

Biophotonics is the interdisciplinary field that explores the interaction of light with biological systems, developing technologies for imaging, diagnostics, and therapies at cellular and molecular levels.

Biomaterials

Biomaterials are substances engineered to interact with biological systems for medical purposes, such as tissue regeneration, prosthetics, and drug delivery, blending materials science with biology and medicine.

Bioinformatics and Computational Biology

Bioinformatics and computational biology leverage computational tools and algorithms to analyze biological data, such as genomes and protein structures, enabling discoveries in genetics, evolution, and personalized medicine.

Biomechanics

Biomechanics is the study of the mechanics of biological systems, examining forces and motions in organisms to understand processes like locomotion, tissue deformation, and cardiovascular dynamics.

Micro and Nanobioengineering

Micro and nanobioengineering focuses on designing and fabricating devices and materials at microscopic and nanoscopic scales, enabling innovations in diagnostics, drug delivery, and cellular manipulation.

Differential Forms

Differential forms provide a powerful mathematical framework for studying calculus on manifolds, generalizing concepts like gradients, curls, and divergence. They are central to fields like geometry, physics, and differential topology, offering a compact way to express integrals and transformations independent of coordinate systems. Their elegance lies in their ability to unify and extend Stokes' theorem across various dimensions.

Tensor Calculus

Tensor calculus is a mathematical tool used to study quantities that transform systematically under coordinate changes. It is crucial in fields like differential geometry and general relativity, where tensors describe curvature, forces, and other physical phenomena. By generalizing vectors and matrices to higher dimensions, tensor calculus provides a versatile language to model and solve problems involving multidimensional spaces.

Complex Analysis

Complex analysis explores the behavior of functions defined on the complex plane. It studies analytic (holomorphic) functions, which are infinitely differentiable and obey the Cauchy-Riemann equations. This field is renowned for its deep theoretical insights and practical applications, from evaluating real integrals to solving problems in physics, engineering, and number theory. Key concepts include contour integration, residue theory, and conformal mappings.

Knot Theory

Knots and Links

A knot is an smooth embedding of the circle (\(S^1\)) into three-dimensional Euclidean space (\(\mathbb{R}^3\)), or the 3-sphere (\(S^3\)), since the 3-sphere is compact. Two knots are defined to be equivalent if there is an ambient isotopy between them.

\[ f: S^1 \hookrightarrow S^3 \quad \text{(or \( \mathbb{R}^3 \))}, \]

where \( f \) is a continuous and differentiable function, and the symbol \( \hookrightarrow \) denotes an embedding.

Two knots \( f, g: S^1 \hookrightarrow S^3 \) are equivalent if there exists an ambient isotopy \( H: S^3 \times [0,1] \to S^3 \) such that:

\[ H(x, 0) = x \quad \text{and} \quad H(f(S^1), 1) = g(S^1), \]

i.e., \( H \) deforms the ambient space \( S^3 \) continuously, transforming the knot \( f(S^1) \) into the knot \( g(S^1) \) without breaking or cutting.

We will not go into much more detail, as these topics are explored further in an introductory course on Algebraic Topology.

In mathematical knot theory, a link is a collection of disjoint knots, i.e., a collection of embeddings \( f_i: S^1 \hookrightarrow S^3 \) for \( i = 1, 2, \ldots, n \), such that the images \( f_i(S^1) \) do not intersect for \( i \neq j \). These components may, however, be linked or knotted together. A knot is a special case of a link consisting of a single component. Links and knots are studied in a branch of mathematics called knot theory.

Implicit in this definition is the existence of a trivial reference link, commonly called the unlink, which consists of a collection of unknots that are pairwise unlinked. However, the term "link" is also sometimes used in contexts where there is no defined notion of a trivial link. For example, a co-dimension 2 link in 3-dimensional space is a subspace of \( \mathbb{R}^3 \) (or often the 3-sphere \( S^3 \)) whose connected components are homeomorphic to \( S^1 \). That is, a link \( L \) in \( \mathbb{R}^3 \) or \( S^3 \) is a subset \[ L = \bigsqcup_{i=1}^n f_i(S^1), \] where \( \bigsqcup \) denotes a disjoint union, and each \( f_i \) is an embedding of the circle \( S^1 \).

The simplest nontrivial example of a link with more than one component is the Hopf link, which consists of two circles (or unknots) linked together exactly once. In mathematical terms, the linking number of the two components is \( \operatorname{Link}(f_1(S^1), f_2(S^1)) = 1 \).

Another notable example is the Borromean rings, a link consisting of three circles. In this case, no two circles are directly linked, but the three components are collectively linked. The Borromean rings form a Brunnian link, meaning that removing any one component results in an unlink. Furthermore, the Borromean rings are the simplest example of such a Brunnian link.

Generalizations

The notion of a link can be generalized in various ways. These generalizations are presented here only as a curiosity, and we will not go into detail.

General manifolds

The term link is sometimes used to describe any submanifold of the sphere \( S^n \) that is diffeomorphic to a disjoint union of a finite number of spheres \( S^j \).

In full generality, the word link is essentially the same as the word knot. Consider a submanifold \( M \) of a manifold \( N \), where \( M \) is trivially embedded in \( N \). A non-trivial embedding of \( M \) in \( N \) (non-trivial in the sense that it is not isotopic to the trivial embedding) can be classified as follows:

  • If \( M \) is disconnected, the embedding is called a link (or said to be linked).
  • If \( M \) is connected, the embedding is called a knot.

Tangles, string links, and braids

In addition to 1-dimensional links (defined as embeddings of circles), it is often useful to consider embedded intervals (or strands), particularly in braid theory. This leads to the notion of a tangle.

Most generally, a tangle is an embedding \[ T: X \to \mathbb{R}^2 \times I, \] where \( X \) is a smooth, compact 1-manifold with boundary \( (X, \partial X) \), and \( I = [0, 1] \). The boundary \( T(\partial X) \) is embedded in \[ \mathbb{R} \times \{0, 1\} \quad (\{0, 1\} = \partial I). \] The type of a tangle is determined by the manifold \( X \) together with a fixed embedding of \( \partial X \).

For further details, see topics such as Tangle (mathematics).

Although explored in greater depth in an Algebraic Topology course, a cobordism between manifolds \( M \) and \( N \) is a compact manifold \( W \) whose boundary is the disjoint union of \( M \) and \( N \), expressed as:

\[ \partial W = M \sqcup N. \]

Knot diagrams

To study knots, it is common to use their projections onto a plane, which are known as knot diagrams.

A knot diagram is a two-dimensional representation of a knot where over-crossings and under-crossings are explicitly indicated. These crossings are depicted using breaks in the curve, showing which strand is "on top" and which is "below." Mathematically, the projection is achieved by mapping the knot in \( \mathbb{R}^3 \) to \( \mathbb{R}^2 \).

Formally, if \( K \) is a knot in \( \mathbb{R}^3 \), its diagram is a projection: \[ \pi: K \to \mathbb{R}^2, \] where crossings are marked to distinguish the over-strand and the under-strand.

Knot diagrams are crucial because they simplify the study of knots, allowing mathematicians to work in two dimensions while preserving the essential properties of the knot.

Reidemeister moves

The Reidemeister moves are a set of three operations that can be applied to knot diagrams. They allow transformations of one diagram into another while preserving the underlying topology of the knot. These moves are fundamental in knot theory because they describe how different diagrams can represent the same knot.

The three Reidemeister moves are:

  2. Type II: Adding or removing two strands that cross over each other (see in the figure).
  3. Type III: Sliding one strand over a crossing of two other strands (see in the figure).

Any two diagrams of the same knot can be related to each other through a finite sequence of these moves. Therefore, the Reidemeister moves provide a foundation for proving that two knots are equivalent or distinct.

Knot invariants

In the mathematical field of knot theory, a knot invariant is a property or quantity associated with a knot that remains unchanged under any deformation of the knot. These deformations include the application of the Reidemeister moves, which are the fundamental operations used to transform knot diagrams without changing the topology of the knot.

Formally, a knot invariant is a function: \[ f: \{\text{knot diagrams}\} \to S, \] where \( S \) is some set (e.g., integers, polynomials, groups), and \( f \) satisfies the property: \[ f(D_1) = f(D_2) \] whenever \( D_1 \) and \( D_2 \) represent the same knot in \( \mathbb{R}^3 \). In other words, the value of the invariant is the same for all diagrams of the same knot.

Knot invariants are powerful tools because they help classify knots and distinguish between knots that might appear similar but are topologically different.

Examples of Knot Invariants

Here are some common examples of knot invariants:

  • Knot Group: The fundamental group of the knot complement, denoted \( \pi_1(S^3 \setminus K) \), is a group that encodes information about the knot.
  • Knot Polynomials: Polynomial invariants, such as:
    • Alexander Polynomial: A polynomial invariant denoted \( \Delta_K(t) \).
    • Jones Polynomial: A polynomial invariant denoted \( V_K(t) \).
  • Knot Signature: A numerical invariant derived from the Seifert matrix of the knot.

Relation to Reidemeister Moves

Since knot invariants are preserved under Reidemeister moves, they serve as a consistent measure of a knot's properties regardless of its diagram representation. This is critical for proving that two knots are the same or different.

For example, if \( f \) is a knot invariant and \( f(D_1) \neq f(D_2) \), then \( D_1 \) and \( D_2 \) cannot represent the same knot.

Knot complement

In mathematics, the knot complement of a tame knot \( K \) (a knot equivalent to a polygonal knot, knots which are not tame are called wild knots) is the space where the knot is not. Informally, it refers to the remaining space in the ambient 3-dimensional manifold after removing the knot and the space immediately surrounding it.

To formalize this, suppose that \( K \) is a knot embedded in a 3-manifold \( M \) (most commonly \( M \) is the 3-sphere, \( S^3 \)). Let \( N \) be a tubular neighborhood of \( K \), so \( N \) is homeomorphic to a solid torus. The knot complement is then defined as: \[ X_K = M - \text{interior}(N), \] where \( \text{interior}(N) \) represents the interior of the tubular neighborhood \( N \).

The knot complement \( X_K \) is a compact 3-manifold, and its boundary, \( \partial X_K \), is homeomorphic to a 2-torus (\( T^2 \)). This means that the boundary of the complement is topologically the same as the boundary of the neighborhood \( N \), which is also a 2-torus.

Relation to Knot Invariants

Many knot invariants are fundamentally invariants of the knot complement. For example:

  • The knot group, \( \pi_1(X_K) \), is the fundamental group of the knot complement.

When the ambient space \( M \) is the 3-sphere (\( S^3 \)), no information is lost by studying the complement rather than the knot itself. The Gordon–Luecke theorem states:

If \( K \) and \( K' \) are two knots with homeomorphic complements, then there exists a homeomorphism of \( S^3 \) that takes \( K \) to \( K' \).

In other words, a knot is uniquely determined by its complement in \( S^3 \).

Generalization to Links

The concept of the knot complement generalizes naturally to link complements. For a link (a collection of knots), the complement is defined analogously by removing the interior of a tubular neighborhood of all the components of the link.

Complements of knots and links are examples of Haken manifolds, which are compact 3-manifolds that satisfy certain topological properties, making them well-suited for analysis in 3-manifold topology.
